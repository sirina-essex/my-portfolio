Ethics in Computing in the Age of Generative AI

This reflective activity explores the fragmented nature of AI ethics governance and the institutional void that shifts responsibility onto individual professionals. Based on critical perspectives from Correa (2023) and Deckard (2023), it proposes a contextualised model for ethical oversight that balances pluralism and enforceability.

Core Challenges in AI Ethics

  • The illusion of a global ethical consensus
  • The unrealistic burden placed on individual professionals
  • The need for situated, pluralistic ethics
  • A structured proposal for context-aware governance

Proposed Model for Ethical Governance

The proposed model is built on three pillars: (1) regional charters aligned with local values, (2) a binding common framework based on fundamental rights, and (3) independent oversight bodies. This hybrid approach reconciles pluralism with coherence and empowers professionals within structured and legitimate ethical systems.

Key Takeaways

  • No universal agreement: ethical principles vary across cultures and regimes.
  • Soft law prevails: most guidelines lack enforcement or oversight.
  • Professionals are overburdened: expectations exceed institutional capacity.
  • Ethics must be collective: situated, deliberative frameworks are essential.
  • Proposal: regional charters, a rights-based legal core, and mixed oversight bodies.

Expected Impacts

  • Legal: Clear obligations transposed into national law with mutual recognition of standards.
  • Professional: Institutional support for technical actors, reducing ethical isolation.
  • Social: Locally co-developed norms enhance inclusion and legitimacy.
  • Ethical: Recognition of value pluralism within coordinated governance frameworks.
