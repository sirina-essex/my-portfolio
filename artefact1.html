Ethics in Computing in the Age of Generative AI

Reflective Activity – Stephanie Irina

1. Background and Context

This reflective activity examines the fragmented nature of AI ethics governance and the institutional vacuum that shifts ethical responsibility onto individual professionals. Drawing from Correa (2023) and Deckard (2023), this work proposes a contextualised model of governance grounded in collective responsibility and enforceable structures.

2. Core Challenges in AI Ethics

  • Fragmented Norms: Ethical standards diverge across jurisdictions and political systems.
  • Soft Law Limitations: Most frameworks lack legal binding and institutional enforcement.
  • Disproportionate Responsibility: Individuals are expected to manage ethical challenges without structural support.
  • Power Asymmetry: Dominant tech actors impose their own standards, undermining user protection.

4. Proposed Model for Ethical Governance

This model is based on three complementary pillars:

  • Regional Charters: Ethical frameworks tailored to local legal and cultural contexts.
  • Binding Common Framework: A shared core based on fundamental rights and legal obligations.
  • Mixed Oversight Bodies: Independent evaluators combining public, private, and academic stakeholders.

5. Key Takeaways

  • Ethical principles are not universal: they must be interpreted within cultural and political contexts.
  • Soft law mechanisms lack teeth and fail to constrain corporate practices.
  • Ethical responsibility should not fall solely on professionals operating in isolation.
  • Ethics requires institutional support, legal grounding, and contextual implementation.

6. Expected Impacts

  • Legal: Formal obligations integrated into national laws, supporting transnational coherence.
  • Professional: Clear ethical standards reduce ambiguity and support practitioners’ decisions.
  • Social: Greater inclusion and legitimacy through co-designed frameworks at local level.
  • Ethical: More balanced frameworks built around diversity and pluralism.
