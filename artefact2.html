Medical Implants: Ethics, Risk, and Governance Failures

This analysis examines a real-world ethical dilemma involving a Bluetooth-enabled cardiac implant developed by Corazon. The device includes a critical cybersecurity flaw—a hardcoded credential—deemed too risky to correct due to surgical constraints. This decision highlights serious ethical, technical, and governance issues in connected healthcare systems.

Identified Ethical Breaches

  • ACM Code Violations: Violations of principles 1.2 (avoid harm), 2.5 (risk evaluation), and 3.7 (critical infrastructure responsibility).
  • Lack of Transparency: Patients were not informed of the vulnerability, breaching principle 1.3 (honesty).
  • BCS Code Failures: All four core duties (public interest, competence, authority, and integrity) were compromised.

Risk Oversight and Technical Concerns

  • Unpatchable Vulnerability: CWE-798 hardcoded credential with no mitigation.
  • No Compensatory Controls: Lack of logging, filtering, or hardware proxies.
  • Ignored Guidance: Non-compliance with FDA (2023) and NIST SP 800-53 standards.

Ethical Reflection

The Corazon case reflects a broader issue: the systemic fragility of ethical governance in medical technologies. By placing the responsibility on individual designers or justifying inaction through medical rationale, the company sidestepped its ethical obligations. As Das et al. (2021) argue, cybersecurity should be treated as essential to patient safety as clinical monitoring.

Conclusion

Ethics in connected healthcare must go beyond intention. Structural support, enforceable standards, and transparency are essential to ensure accountability and patient safety. This case underscores the need for integrating security by design and robust oversight from the earliest stages of development.
