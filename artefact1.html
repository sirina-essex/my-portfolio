Ethics in Computing in the Age of Generative AI

This reflective analysis examines the ethical challenges raised by generative AI systems, based on the critical perspectives of Correa (2023) and Deckard (2023). It highlights fragmented norms, institutional weaknesses, and the over-reliance on individual responsibility, proposing an alternative model of contextualised and collective ethical governance.

Core Challenges in AI Ethics

  • Fragmented Norms: Ethical frameworks differ across political systems, masking deep cultural and legal divergences (Correa, 2023).
  • Soft Law Limitations: Most governance texts rely on non-binding declarations, without enforcement or oversight mechanisms.
  • Disproportionate Responsibility: Professionals are expected to navigate complex ethical decisions without institutional support (Deckard, 2023).
  • Power Asymmetry: Dominant tech companies shape ethics to their advantage, reinforcing user vulnerability (Garcia et al., 2014).

Limits of Individual-Centric Approaches

Deckard proposes a model of ethical integration via multi-skilled professionals. However, critics such as Canvat (2020) and Floridi (2019) argue that expecting isolated individuals to bear ethical responsibility in distributed systems is unrealistic and structurally unsupported.

Proposed Model for Ethical Governance

The proposed model is built on three pillars: (1) regional charters aligned with local values, (2) a binding common framework based on fundamental rights, and (3) independent oversight bodies. This hybrid approach reconciles pluralism with coherence and empowers professionals within structured and legitimate ethical systems.

Expected Impacts

  • Legal: Clear obligations transposed into national law with mutual recognition of standards.
  • Professional: Institutional support for technical actors, reducing ethical isolation.
  • Social: Locally co-developed norms enhance inclusion and legitimacy.
  • Ethical: Recognition of value pluralism within coordinated governance frameworks.

Broader Implications for Research and Policy

This analysis calls for shifting the ethical debate from abstract universalism to situated, structured, and deliberative models. Ethics should not be reduced to a declarative intention, but grounded in enforceable mechanisms that reflect the political, cultural, and legal diversity of contemporary societies.
